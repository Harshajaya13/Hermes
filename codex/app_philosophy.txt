Not because it is productive.
Because it is growth.

Notice the common pattern?
Everything passes through reflection.
Reflection is like a compiler.
It transforms experience into something.
So reflection is probably the engine, not the data model.
I think Hermes has something nobody else has.
Let's ignore software for a second.
Imagine yourself in 2035.
You open Hermes.
You don't care that you solved 5,000 math problems.
You care about moments like these:
"This betting problem changed how I think about probability."
"This startup article completely changed how I see product design."
"Today I realized why streaks never worked for me."
Those are not tasks.
Not notes.
Not commits.
They are...
transformations.
So I started asking myself...
What does Hermes actually preserve?
Not information.
Not completion.
It preserves...
change.
Every time something changes inside you...
Hermes records it.
That is incredibly different.
Hermes wouldn't care that you read 300 articles.Hermes would care that
Article #27 changed how you think.
Or maybe...
This is even stronger.
Evolution
Every meaningful change becomes an Evolution.
Now Hermes literally stores your evolution.
even a completely original term.
That's actually how memorable software is built.
Git has commits.
Discord has servers.
Reddit has subreddits.
Hermes shouldn't borrow someone else's language if it has a better one.
Here's the thing I would protect.
I would not make the core metric:
"How much did you do?"
I also would not make it:
"How much did you reflect?"
I would make it:
"How much did you change?"
Because that's the outcome.
Reflection is the process.
Growth is the destination.
Change is the evidence.
I have one question for you.
Imagine it's 2036.
You've used Hermes every day for 10 years.
You open one screen.
What do you most want to see?
Is it:
Every question you solved?
Every article you read?
Every commit you made?
Every reflection you wrote?
Or every moment where you became a slightly different person than yesterday?
