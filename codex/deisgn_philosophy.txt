Chapter 1
Visual Philosophy

For example:

What should Hermes feel like?
Why OLED black?
Why rounded corners?
Why minimal?
Why no visual clutter?

I already know one sentence belongs here:

The interface should disappear so the user's thinking becomes the focus.

Chapter 2
Color Philosophy

Not hex codes.

Questions like:

Why OLED black?
Why muted colors?
Why avoid bright reds?
When should color mean importance?
When should gray be used?
Chapter 3
Typography Philosophy

Not fonts.

Questions like:

What should be loud?
What should whisper?
How many font sizes exist?
Should numbers stand out?
Should reflections feel like reading a book?
Chapter 4
Card Philosophy

This is huge.

You already hate giant cards 😂

We need rules like:

Cards never dominate the screen.
Cards group meaning, not decoration.
Cards should feel lightweight.
Cards should never hide important information.
Every card must have one clear purpose.
Chapter 5
Icon Philosophy

Questions:

Should every Block have an icon?

Should icons always be outlined?

Should icons carry meaning or just decoration?

Should emojis ever be allowed?

Chapter 6
Motion Language

We already discussed UX.

Now we define the visual language.

Examples:

Fade
Slide
Scale
Ripple
Haptic timing

Not because animations look cool.

Because they explain state changes.

Chapter 7
Spacing Philosophy

This is the chapter almost nobody writes.

Yet it makes premium software.

Questions like:

Why does TickTick feel breathable?
Why does Arc Browser feel calm?
Why do Apple apps feel expensive?

The answer is usually...

Spacing.

Not gradients.

Chapter 8
Empty Space

I want an entire chapter.

Because...

Empty space is not wasted space.

It lets the mind breathe.

One of the reasons your first UI felt bad wasn't because it had empty space.

It had unintentional empty space.

There's a difference.

Intentional whitespace guides the eye.

Accidental emptiness feels unfinished.

Chapter 9
Component Consistency

Rules like:

Every button behaves the same.
Every dialog looks the same.
Every sheet opens the same.
Every list scrolls the same.

Consistency builds trust.

Chapter 10
Emotional Aesthetics

This is my favorite chapter.

Hermes shouldn't look futuristic.

It shouldn't look "AI."

It shouldn't look like a gaming app.

It should feel...

Calm
Quiet
Personal
Trustworthy
Warm
Timeless

If someone opens Hermes in 2036, it should still feel modern.

But before we write anything...

I want to ask you one question.

Not about colors.

Not about fonts.

Close your eyes and imagine this.

It's 10:45 PM.

You studied.

You reflected.

You recorded an Evolutio.

You close Hermes.

What should you feel in that exact moment?

Not what you see.

Not what the app says.

What emotion should stay with you after the screen turns off?

Hermes is trying to make them feel:

"Today mattered."

That's a completely different emotional target.
Hermes should feel like

A premium notebook crafted by someone who deeply respects your thoughts.

Apple Notes.

None of them scream.

I think we need Spacing Laws.

Not values.

Laws.

For example:

Law 1

Every element must have room to breathe.

Law 2

Empty space is an active design element, not leftover space.

Law 3

Related things stay visually close.

Unrelated things stay visually apart.

Law 4

No screen should feel empty.

No screen should feel crowded.

There is a balance.

Law 5

This one is my favorite.

The eye should always know where to look next.

If users stop and wonder

"Where do I click?"

The layout has already failed.

Law 6

The most important action should always be visible without searching.

Law 7

Density should follow importance.

Today's Question gets more space.

Settings get less.

Law 8

Visual weight should match conceptual weight.

For example...

Math Block.

Large.

Delete Account.

Tiny inside Settings.

Because conceptually it's rare.

Typography should be decided before implementation.

Not the font.

The hierarchy.

For example:

Screen Title

↓

Section Title

↓

Block Title

↓

Item Title

↓

Body

↓

Metadata

That hierarchy never changes.

Whether you use Inter, Roboto, or SF Pro later doesn't matter.

The relationships stay the same.

I think we've reached something interesting.

The Codex is slowly splitting into two layers.

Invisible Design

This is what we've been writing.

Philosophy
UX
Spacing
Trust
Hierarchy
Navigation
Emotional Design

Users never consciously notice these.

Yet they determine whether Hermes feels calming or exhausting.

Visible Design

This comes later.

Colors
Fonts
Icons
Borders
Shadows
Radius
Material 3
Components

These are the things people point at.

Ironically, they're usually less important than the invisible design.
